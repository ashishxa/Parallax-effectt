Kratos, born in Sparta, was trained from childhood to be a brutal warrior. His life was defined by war, hardship, and the unrelenting Spartan discipline that shaped his ferocious nature.

In desperation during battle, Kratos pledged himself to Ares, the God of War. The deal granted him immense power—but at a great cost. Kratos unknowingly slaughtered his own wife and daughter under Ares’ manipulation.

ursed by his past, Kratos became known as the "Ghost of Sparta," with ash-white skin from the ashes of his dead family, fused to him as a mark of his guilt and rage.

Betrayed by Ares and later manipulated by other Olympian gods, Kratos turned against the entire Greek pantheon. His vengeance was merciless and consumed all who crossed him.

Kratos waged war against the gods themselves. He defeated Ares, Poseidon, Hades, Zeus, and many others—causing chaos and destruction across Greece in his quest for justice and redemption.

After the collapse of Olympus, Kratos sought a new life. He traveled far north into the realm of Norse mythology, hiding his godhood and trying to live a life of peace.

Kratos became a father again to a boy named Atreus. Struggling to tame his rage and be a better man, he raised his son in secret, teaching him survival—and discipline.

As they fulfilled the dying wish of Atreus’s mother to scatter her ashes from the highest peak, Kratos and Atreus uncovered secrets about the boy’s true identity and a prophecy tied to Ragnarok.

he Norse gods, including Baldur, Thor, and Odin, began to see Kratos and Atreus as a threat. Battles with Baldur and others pulled Kratos back into a world of divine war and destiny.

With Ragnarok looming, Kratos must face his past and future. As Atreus learns the truth about himself, father and son must decide whether to follow fate—or forge a new path for all the realms.
